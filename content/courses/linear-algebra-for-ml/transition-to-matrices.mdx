# 2.3 Transition to Matrices

Up to now, we have written out our equations line-by-line using variables like $x$ and $y$. But in machine learning, we deal with millions of data points and features. Writing out equations by hand is impossible! 

To handle large-scale data, we pack our numbers into grids called **Matrices**. In this lecture, we will learn how to rewrite systems of equations in matrix form ($A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$) and master the core rules of **Matrix Multiplication**.

---

## 1. Moving from Equations to Matrices ($A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$)

Let's take a standard system of linear equations:

$$2x_1 + 3x_2 = 12$$
$$x_1 - 2x_2 = -1$$

We can separate this system into three compact parts:
1. **$A$ (Coefficient Matrix):** A grid containing only the coefficients (the numbers in front of the variables).
   $$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$$
2. **$\mathbf{x}$ (Variable Vector):** A column vector of our unknown variables.
   $$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix}$$
3. **$\mathbf{b}$ (Target Vector):** A column vector containing the answers on the right side of the equals sign.
   $$\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Now, we can write the entire system of equations in a single, elegant line of matrix algebra:

$$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$$

---

## 2. Matrix-Vector Multiplication: How It Works

To make sure $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is actually the same as our original equations, we need to know how to multiply a matrix by a vector.

### The Rule: Row Dot Column
To multiply a matrix by a vector, we take each **row** of the matrix and compute its **dot product** with the column vector.

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} (2 \times x_1) + (3 \times x_2) \\ (1 \times x_1) + (-2 \times x_2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2x_1 + 3x_2 \\ x_1 - 2x_2 \end{bmatrix}$$

This yields a new column vector. Matching this result to our target vector $\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}$ gives us our original equations back!

---

## 3. Matrix-Matrix Multiplication

In machine learning, we often multiply matrices by other matrices (for example, when passing a batch of inputs through a neural network layer).

Suppose we want to multiply matrix $A$ (size $2 \times 2$) by matrix $B$ (size $2 \times 2$) to get a new matrix $C = AB$:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{bmatrix}$$

We calculate each cell in the result matrix $C$ using the **Row-by-Column** rule:

* **Row 1 of A $\times$ Column 1 of B $\implies$ Cell $C_{11}$:**
  $$(1 \times 5) + (2 \times 7) = 5 + 14 = 19$$
* **Row 1 of A $\times$ Column 2 of B $\implies$ Cell $C_{12}$:**
  $$(1 \times 6) + (2 \times 8) = 6 + 16 = 22$$
* **Row 2 of A $\times$ Column 1 of B $\implies$ Cell $C_{21}$:**
  $$(3 \times 5) + (4 \times 7) = 15 + 28 = 43$$
* **Row 2 of A $\times$ Column 2 of B $\implies$ Cell $C_{22}$:**
  $$(3 \times 6) + (4 \times 8) = 18 + 32 = 50$$

So, our final result is:
$$AB = \begin{bmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{bmatrix}$$

> [!WARNING]
> **Order Matters!** In regular math, $2 \times 3 = 3 \times 2$. But in matrix multiplication, **$AB \neq BA$** in general! Changing the order of multiplication will yield a completely different result, or might even be mathematically impossible because the sizes don't match.
